Touring on bikes can be a memorable individual experience. Several hours in the saddle moving the legs in rhythm with the breath is amenable to the types of meditative states achieved by dzogchen masters. For me, this is an effective method for spiritual growth. For others, it sounds like a particularly unappealing version of hell.

I set a course with a few goals in mind:

0. Have fun
1. Bring my family together for the first time in ten years
2. Share my passion for cycling (get people on bikes)
3. See a familiar place in a new way

I had ten or more selfish reasons to plan this trip. I wanted to get in one more bike tour before the year was up and I chose the most beautiful and rugged place I could think of with the most pristine geology. Nested among National Parks and wilderness areas, famous red-rock landscapes, and steep climbs, Southwest Utah was the perfect candidate.

The original course was a 400-mile unsupported solo adventure---a point-to-point epic that was going to take me to my mental and physical limits. This changed after a conversation with my cousin, Cassidy.

I can't remember how it got on her radar, but she wanted a piece of the action. The course slowly changed as more of my family signed up to suffer in the desert. I had mixed feelings of excitement and disappointment. I wanted to ride with my family, but I knew this meant that the "adventure dial" had to be turned down, as I saw it. The course had to be shortened. The climbs abbreviated. It was too risky to go unsupported with riders of mixed abilities. This wasn't my trip anymore and I had to come to terms with that.

A silver-lining of the pandemic allowed us to book a cabin at a [guest ranch](https://bouldermountainguestranch.com/) in Boulder, Utah for pennies. From our home base we spent three days being humbled by the desert's magnificent cliffs, box canyons, and by heroic efforts up Hell's Backbone. Two E-bikes and a magical rainbow school bus (shout out [Fuego Sol](https://www.instagram.com/fuegosol_official/?hl=en)) allowed for everyone to experience the exposure without months of training. This was a huge blessing in my mind. Never did I expect to find myself climbing up double-digit grades to 9200' with my family whose cycling experience ranged from hard-core racers to fair-weather beach cruisers.

In the end, giving up an epic unsupported adventure for a shared touring experience with my family was the best decision. It happened seemingly by chance, or perhaps it was guided by something bigger than any of us.

Much love. Go ride bikes.
